Unit 5: Stoichiometry and Reactions

Section 1: Chemical Equations and Conservation of Mass

Writing and Balancing Chemical Equations

Components of a Chemical Equation

  • Reactants → Products
  • States of matter: (s), (l), (g), (aq)
  • Coefficients indicate relative amounts

Balancing Steps

  1. Write unbalanced equation with correct formulas
  2. Count atoms of each element
  3. Add coefficients (never change subscripts)
  4. Verify equal atoms on both sides

Types of Equations

1. Complete (Molecular)

  • Shows full chemical formulas
  • Includes state symbols
  • Example: 2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l)

2. Ionic

  • Shows dissolved ions
  • Shows spectator ions
  • Example: Na⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) + Ag⁺(aq) + NO₃⁻(aq) → AgCl(s) + Na⁺(aq) + NO₃⁻(aq)

3. Net Ionic

  • Omits spectator ions
  • Shows only species that change
  • Example: Ag⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) → AgCl(s)

Conservation of Mass

Macroscopic Level

  • Total mass of reactants = Total mass of products
  • No atoms created or destroyed

Atomic Level

  • Number of each type of atom conserved
  • Only bonds are rearranged

Section 2: Basic Stoichiometric Calculations

Mass-Mole Relationships

Conversions

  • Moles to mass: moles × molar mass
  • Mass to moles: mass ÷ molar mass
  • Atoms to moles: atoms ÷ Avogadro's number

Mass Percent Calculations

Mass % = (mass of element/total mass) × 100

Empirical and Molecular Formulas

Determining Empirical Formula

  1. Convert mass % to mass
  2. Convert mass to moles
  3. Find simplest whole-number ratio

Molecular Formula

  • Multiple of empirical formula
  • Determined using molar mass
  • Molecular formula = (Empirical formula)n

Combustion Analysis

  • Used to determine empirical formula
  • Analyzes CO₂ and H₂O produced
  • Calculate mass of C, H from products

Section 3: Stoichiometric Relationships

Molar Ratios

Using Balanced Equations

  • Coefficients show molar ratios
  • Used to convert between reactants/products
  • Moles A × (moles B/moles A) = moles B

Limiting Reactant

  • Determines maximum product
  • Reactant used up first
  • Other reactants in excess

Theoretical vs Actual Yield

Theoretical: maximum possible product
Actual: amount actually produced
Percent yield = (actual/theoretical) × 100
Percent error = |(actual - theoretical)/theoretical| × 100

Section 4: Types of Reactions

Common Reaction Types

1. Synthesis (Combination)

  • A + B → AB
  • Elements combine to form compound

2. Decomposition

  • AB → A + B
  • Compound breaks into elements/simpler compounds

3. Single Replacement

  • A + BC → AC + B
  • One element replaces another

4. Double Replacement (Metathesis)

  • AB + CD → AD + CB
  • Ions exchange partners

5. Combustion

  • Substance + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O (+ other oxides)
  • Complete oxidation with oxygen

Evidence of Chemical Reactions

Physical Signs

  • Color change
  • Gas formation (bubbles)
  • Precipitate formation
  • Temperature change
  • Light/sound production

Chemical Evidence

  • pH changes
  • New compound formation
  • Energy changes
Key Concepts for AP Exam:
  • Master balancing equations and reaction types
  • Understand stoichiometric relationships
  • Be able to identify limiting reactants
  • Calculate theoretical and percent yields
  • Recognize evidence of chemical reactions
