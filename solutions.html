Unit 4: Solutions

Section 1: Basic Solution Concepts

Fundamental Definitions

  1. Solution: Homogeneous mixture of two or more substances
  2. Solute: Substance being dissolved (usually smaller amount)
  3. Solvent: Substance doing the dissolving (usually larger amount)

Types of Solutions

1. Electrolyte Solutions

  • Conduct electricity when dissolved
  • Form ions in solution
  • Examples: NaCl, HCl, KOH

2. Non-electrolyte Solutions

  • Don't conduct electricity
  • No ion formation
  • Examples: Sugar, alcohol in water

3. Based on Concentration

  • Unsaturated: Can dissolve more solute
  • Saturated: Maximum solute dissolved at given temperature
  • Supersaturated: Contains more solute than saturated solution (unstable)

Section 2: Solution Concentrations

Concentration Units

1. Molarity (M)

M = moles solute/liters solution
  • Most common unit
  • Temperature dependent

2. Molality (m)

m = moles solute/kg solvent
  • Temperature independent
  • Used for colligative properties

3. Mass Percent

% = (mass solute/mass solution) × 100

4. Mole Fraction (X)

X = moles component/total moles
  • Sum of all mole fractions = 1

Concentration Calculations

Converting Between Units

  • Need density for molarity ↔ molality
  • Need molar mass for mass% ↔ molarity

General Steps:

  1. Write known information
  2. Identify target unit
  3. Use dimensional analysis

Serial Dilutions

M₁V₁ = M₂V₂
  • Used to make less concentrated solutions
  • Common in lab work

Section 3: Solution Formation Process

Dissolution Process Steps

1. Separation of Solute Particles

  • Endothermic
  • Breaking solute-solute interactions
  • Energy required (ΔH₁)

2. Separation of Solvent Particles

  • Endothermic
  • Breaking solvent-solvent interactions
  • Energy required (ΔH₂)

3. Solute-Solvent Interaction

  • Exothermic
  • Formation of new bonds
  • Energy released (ΔH₃)

Energy of Solution

ΔH solution = ΔH₁ + ΔH₂ + ΔH₃
  • If ΔH solution < 0: Exothermic dissolution
  • If ΔH solution > 0: Endothermic dissolution

Section 4: Factors Affecting Solubility

Structural Factors

1. Polarity

  • "Like dissolves like"
  • Polar dissolves polar
  • Nonpolar dissolves nonpolar

2. Intermolecular Forces

  • Stronger IMFs = lower solubility
  • Types involved:
    • Hydrogen bonding
    • Dipole-dipole
    • London dispersion

Physical Factors

1. Temperature

  • Generally increases solubility of solids
  • Decreases solubility of gases
  • Affects saturation point

2. Pressure

  • Mainly affects gas solubility
  • Higher pressure = higher solubility
  • Follows Henry's Law

3. Surface Area

  • Larger surface area = faster dissolution
  • Doesn't affect final solubility

Section 5: Solution Properties

Vapor Pressure

  • Decreases with addition of nonvolatile solute
  • Raoult's Law: P solution = X solvent × P°solvent
  • Related to boiling point elevation

Physical Properties Changed by Solutes

1. Boiling Point

  • Increases with solute addition
  • ΔTb = Kb × m (molality)

2. Melting Point

  • Decreases with solute addition
  • ΔTf = Kf × m (molality)

Effect of Solutes on IMFs

1. Ion-Dipole Forces

  • Form between ions and polar solvent
  • Stronger than original solvent IMFs

2. Changes in Original IMFs

  • Disruption of solvent-solvent interactions
  • Formation of new solute-solvent interactions
Key Concepts for AP Exam:
  • Master concentration calculations and unit conversions
  • Understand solution formation process and energetics
  • Know factors affecting solubility
  • Understand colligative properties
  • Be able to predict solubility based on molecular structure
