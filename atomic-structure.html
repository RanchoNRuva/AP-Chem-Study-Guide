Unit 1: Atomic Structure & Quantum Theory

Section 1: Fundamental Concepts and Atomic Theory

Basic Definitions

  • Atom: The smallest particle of an element that retains its chemical properties
  • Element: Pure substance made of only one type of atom
  • Molecule: Two or more atoms held together by chemical bonds
  • Formula unit: The simplest ratio of ions in an ionic compound
  • Ion: An atom or molecule with a positive or negative charge due to gaining or losing electrons

Development of Atomic Theory

Dalton's Atomic Theory:
  • Matter is made up of indivisible atoms
  • All atoms of an element are identical
  • Atoms combine in whole-number ratios to form compounds
  • Chemical reactions rearrange atoms (no creation/destruction)
  • Later modified after discovery of subatomic particles

Key Experimental Discoveries

  1. Thomson's Experiment (1897)
    • Discovered electrons using cathode ray tubes
    • Proposed "plum pudding" model of the atom
    • Demonstrated electrons had negative charge
  2. Millikan's Oil Drop Experiment
    • Determined charge of electron (1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C)
    • Demonstrated quantization of electric charge
    • Enabled calculation of electron mass
  3. Rutherford's Gold Foil Experiment
    • Discovered dense, positively charged nucleus
    • Most of atom is empty space
    • Led to nuclear model of the atom
    • Disproved Thomson's model

Section 2: Subatomic Particles and Isotopes

Fundamental Particles

Particle Charge Mass (g) Location
Electron -1 9.109 × 10⁻²⁸ Electron cloud
Proton +1 1.673 × 10⁻²⁴ Nucleus
Neutron 0 1.675 × 10⁻²⁴ Nucleus

Isotopes and Mass

  • Isotope: Atoms with same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons
  • Atomic Number (Z): Number of protons
  • Mass Number (A): Total protons + neutrons
  • Calculations:
    • Protons = Atomic number
    • Neutrons = Mass number - Atomic number
    • Electrons (neutral atom) = Protons

Mass Spectrometry and Average Atomic Mass

Average atomic mass = Σ(isotopic mass × fractional abundance)

Section 3: Electromagnetic Radiation and Quantum Mechanics

Wave Properties

  • Wavelength (λ): Distance between wave peaks
  • Frequency (ν): Waves passing a point per second
  • Speed (c): Speed of light (3.0 × 10⁸ m/s)
  • Key equation: c = λν

Energy Relationships

Planck's equation: E = hν

Where h = Planck's constant (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s)

Energy and wavelength: E = hc/λ

Quantum Numbers

  1. Principal (n):
    • Energy level
    • Values: 1, 2, 3...
  2. Angular Momentum (l):
    • Orbital shape
    • Values: 0 to (n-1)
    • Labels: s, p, d, f
  3. Magnetic (ml):
    • Orbital orientation
    • Values: -l to +l
  4. Spin (ms):
    • Electron spin
    • Values: +½ or -½

Section 4: Electron Configuration

Electron Configuration Rules

  1. Aufbau Principle: Fill lowest energy orbitals first
  2. Pauli Exclusion: Max two electrons per orbital with opposite spins
  3. Hund's Rule: Electrons in equal-energy orbitals spread out before pairing

Writing Electron Configurations

  • Format: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ etc.
  • Can use noble gas core notation
  • Example: Na = [Ne]3s¹
Common Mistakes to Avoid:
  • Forgetting to check for exceptions (e.g., Cr, Cu)
  • Incorrect orbital filling order
  • Wrong number of electrons in subshells
  • Not considering ion charge when writing configurations

Photoelectron Spectroscopy (PES)

  • Understanding PES Data:
    • Measures electron binding energies
    • Higher binding energy = closer to nucleus
    • Peak intensity proportional to number of electrons
  • Interpreting PES Spectra:
    • Each peak represents different energy level
    • Area under peak shows number of electrons
    • Higher energy peaks = core electrons
    • Lower energy peaks = valence electrons
Key Concepts for AP Exam:
  • Know all major atomic models and their historical development
  • Understand quantum numbers and their significance
  • Master electron configuration rules and exceptions
  • Be able to interpret PES data
  • Understand the relationship between electron configuration and periodic trends
