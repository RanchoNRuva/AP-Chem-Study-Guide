Unit 2: Chemical Bonding

Section 1: Fundamentals of Chemical Bonding

Types of Chemical Bonds

  1. Ionic Bonding
    • Transfer of electrons between metals and nonmetals
    • Forms between elements with large electronegativity differences
    • Results in formation of charged ions
  2. Covalent Bonding
    • Sharing of electrons between nonmetals
    • Forms between elements with similar electronegativity
    • Can be polar or nonpolar
  3. Metallic Bonding
    • Sharing of delocalized electrons among metal atoms
    • "Sea of electrons" model
    • Explains electrical conductivity and malleability

Electronegativity and Bond Character

  • Electronegativity: Tendency of an atom to attract shared electrons
  • Electronegativity Trends:
    • Increases left to right across period
    • Increases bottom to top in group
    • Fluorine most electronegative (4.0)
  • Bond Character Determination:
    ΔEN < 0.4: Nonpolar covalent
    0.4 ≤ ΔEN ≤ 1.7: Polar covalent
    ΔEN > 1.7: Ionic

Section 2: Ionic Bonding

Properties of Ionic Compounds

  1. High melting and boiling points
  2. Brittle crystalline solids
  3. Conduct electricity when molten or dissolved
  4. Form crystal lattice structures
  5. Usually soluble in polar solvents

Lattice Energy

Definition: Energy required to separate ionic solid into gaseous ions
Coulomb's Law: E = k(Q₁Q₂)/r²
k = Coulomb's constant
Q₁,Q₂ = ion charges
r = distance between ions

Factors Affecting Lattice Energy

  • Higher charges = stronger lattice energy
  • Smaller ions = stronger lattice energy

Section 3: Covalent Bonding

Lewis Electron-Dot Structures

Drawing Rules:
  • Show valence electrons as dots
  • Place dots around atomic symbol
  • Maximum of 8 electrons (octet rule)
  • Shared pairs shown as lines

Octet Rule and Exceptions

  • Octet Rule: Atoms tend to gain/lose/share electrons to achieve 8 valence electrons
  • Exceptions:
    • H, Li, Be (less than octet)
    • B (incomplete octet)
    • Elements period 3 and below (expanded octet)

Bond Types and Characteristics

Bond Type Shared Pairs Bond Strength Bond Length
Single Bond One Weakest Longest
Double Bond Two Intermediate Intermediate
Triple Bond Three Strongest Shortest

Section 4: Advanced Bonding Concepts

VSEPR Theory

Key Geometries:
  1. Linear (2 domains): 180°
  2. Trigonal planar (3 domains): 120°
  3. Tetrahedral (4 domains): 109.5°
  4. Trigonal bipyramidal (5 domains): 120°, 90°
  5. Octahedral (6 domains): 90°

Hybridization

  • Types:
    • sp³: Tetrahedral geometry
    • sp²: Trigonal planar geometry
    • sp: Linear geometry
  • Determination:
    • Count electron domains around central atom
    • Consider molecular geometry

Sigma and Pi Bonds

Sigma (σ) Bonds:
  • Head-on overlap
  • All single bonds
  • Stronger than pi bonds
Pi (π) Bonds:
  • Parallel overlap
  • In multiple bonds
  • Weaker than sigma bonds

Resonance Structures

  • Definition: Multiple Lewis structures for same molecule
  • Rules:
    • Same atomic positions
    • Only electron positions differ
    • Equal bond orders in all structures
  • Bond Length: Average of all resonance forms

Formal Charge

FC = V - N - B
V = valence electrons
N = nonbonding electrons
B = bonding electrons/2
  • Uses:
    • Determine most stable Lewis structure
    • Predict reactive sites
    • Explain molecular behavior
Key Concepts for AP Exam:
  • Master drawing Lewis structures and determining molecular geometry
  • Understand relationships between bond order, length, and strength
  • Know how to determine hybridization and bond types
  • Be able to identify and explain resonance structures
  • Calculate and interpret formal charges
