Unit 3: Solids, Liquids, & Gases

Section 1: Types of Solids

Ionic Crystalline Solids

Properties:

  • High melting/boiling points
  • Brittle
  • Poor electrical conductivity as solids
  • Good electrical conductivity when molten/dissolved
  • Hard crystalline structure

Atomic Explanation:

  • Strong electrostatic forces between ions
  • Regular 3D lattice arrangement
  • Fixed positions of ions

Metallic Solids

Properties:

  • High electrical conductivity
  • High thermal conductivity
  • Malleable and ductile
  • Lustrous appearance
  • Variable hardness

Atomic Explanation:

  • "Sea of electrons" around positive metal ions
  • Delocalized electrons enable conductivity
  • Mobile electrons allow shape changes without breaking

Covalent Network Solids

Properties:

  • Very high melting points
  • Extremely hard
  • Variable electrical conductivity
  • Insoluble in most solvents

Atomic Explanation:

  • Extensive networks of covalent bonds
  • Strong bonds in all directions
  • Examples: diamond, graphite, silicon

Molecular Solids

Properties:

  • Low melting/boiling points
  • Soft
  • Poor electrical conductivity
  • Often volatile

Atomic Explanation:

  • Held by intermolecular forces (IMFs)
  • Weak forces between molecules
  • Molecules retain individual identity

Section 2: Intermolecular Forces (IMFs)

Types of IMFs

1. London Dispersion Forces

  • Present between all molecules
  • Caused by temporary dipoles
  • Strength increases with:
    • Molecular size
    • Surface area
    • Polarizability

2. Dipole-Dipole Forces

  • Between polar molecules
  • Stronger than dispersion forces
  • Depends on molecular polarity

3. Hydrogen Bonding

  • Between H and F, O, or N
  • Strongest IMF
  • Special case of dipole-dipole
  • Affects boiling points and solubility

Relative Strengths of IMFs

Type of IMF Strength (kJ/mol) Characteristics
Dispersion Forces 0.1-30 Always present, increase with molecular size
Dipole-Dipole 2-15 Requires polar molecules
Hydrogen Bonding 10-40 Strongest IMF, specific to H, N, O, F

Section 3: Liquid Properties and Phase Changes

Liquid Properties

Surface Tension

  • Caused by IMFs at surface
  • Stronger IMFs = higher surface tension
  • Water has high surface tension (H-bonding)

Viscosity

  • Resistance to flow
  • Increases with stronger IMFs
  • Decreases with temperature

Capillary Action

  • Rise of liquids in narrow tubes
  • Balance of adhesive and cohesive forces
  • Stronger in polar liquids

Phase Changes

Phase Change Energies:

  • Fusion (melting): ΔHfus
  • Vaporization: ΔHvap
  • Sublimation: ΔHsub
  • Energy absorbed during phase changes

Vapor Pressure

Definition

Pressure of vapor in equilibrium with liquid

Affecting Factors:

  • Temperature (increases VP)
  • IMF strength (decreases VP)
  • Surface area (no effect on VP)

Section 4: Gas Laws and Properties

Basic Gas Laws

Boyle's Law: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ (constant T, n)

Charles' Law: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ (constant P, n)

Avogadro's Law: V₁/n₁ = V₂/n₂ (constant P, T)

Combined Gas Law: P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂

Ideal Gas Law: PV = nRT

Real vs Ideal Gases

Ideal Gas Assumptions:

  • No particle volume
  • No IMFs
  • Elastic collisions
  • Random motion

Real Gas Behavior:

  • Particles have volume
  • Have IMFs
  • Non-elastic collisions
  • Van der Waals equation accounts for deviations

Kinetic Molecular Theory

Key Concepts:

  • Particles in constant motion
  • Energy proportional to temperature
  • Collisions are elastic
  • No IMFs between particles

Root Mean Square Speed:

urms = √(3RT/M)
  • Relates to temperature and molar mass
  • Measure of average molecular speed

Gas Diffusion and Effusion

Diffusion:

Mixing of gases

Effusion:

Gas passing through small hole

Graham's Law:

rate₁/rate₂ = √(M₂/M₁)

  • Master gas law calculations
  • Understand phase changes and energy involved
  • Know how to calculate vapor pressure
  • Understand gas behavior deviations from ideal
  • Be able to use kinetic molecular theory to explain gas properties
  • Master gas diffusion and effusion calculations
  • Know how to interpret phase diagrams
  • Understand relationship between IMFs and physical properties
  • Be able to explain liquid properties using IMFs
  • Know how to predict relative strengths of IMFs based on molecular structure
